1. Press release (draft — the ending we're writing toward)

TimelinesAI launches Timelines CRM: run your sales inside WhatsApp

[launch date] — TimelinesAI today released Timelines CRM (crm.timelines.ai), the simplest way for a small business that sells on WhatsApp to stop losing deals in chats.

Most small businesses in WhatsApp-heavy markets run their entire sales process inside WhatsApp — and nowhere else. Leads, follow-ups, and closed deals live in an endless chat list. Classic CRMs don't help: they are built email-first, cost real money, and demand a workflow these teams will never adopt. The result is lost follow-ups and lost revenue.

Timelines CRM starts where these businesses already are. Scan a QR code with the WhatsApp number you already use — no API, no approvals, no setup call. Every conversation becomes a contact automatically. Drag chats through a simple pipeline, set follow-up reminders, answer with templates, and let AI summarize conversations and fill in deal details for you. A team of one to five sellers is fully working in one sitting.

Timelines CRM is included for TimelinesAI customers and available standalone with a free trial. Businesses that outgrow it and adopt a full CRM stay in the family: connecting WhatsApp to Pipedrive, HubSpot and 20+ other CRMs is what TimelinesAI's main product does today.

2. FAQ — the hard questions, answered in advance

Why a separate app and not a feature inside TimelinesAI?

Speed and fit. Anything inside the core product means our release cycle, our legacy UI, and roughly a year of calendar time. And this is not a new WhatsApp product — but let's be precise about what's reused and what's net-new, because the sizing depends on it. Reused from the platform: QR connection, session management, two-way message sync with history, and the workspace billing rail — the hard, risky WhatsApp infrastructure. Net-new build: the CRM application itself — data model and storage for deals/contacts/fields, the UI, self-serve onboarding, the addon billing SKU, and the AI field-extraction pipeline. The integration seam is proven (10+ integrations built on it in the past month, 3 listed live), but an integration is a stateless adapter and this is a stateful app — the analogy covers the seam, not the whole build. Two steps: a throwaway prototype on Calypso (the rapid-build platform agreed 20.07) to validate the happy path AND to reality-check this sizing — small: our estimate is 3–5 working days for a clickable happy path (QR-connected workspace, real chats via the seam, auto-contacts, pipeline, deal record) plus 2–3 days for the AI field-extraction quality eval on ~50 real conversations. Then a production build of the Must set in Section 12 — medium, revisited after the prototype; if the honest answer comes back large, that surfaces before money is spent, not after. "Launched" means: a stranger can sign up, connect, pay, and get help without any of us in the loop. The core product serves "I have a CRM" users; this serves "I will never have a CRM" users.

Who owns it?

The #1 decision this meeting must ratify (Section 11), and a hard gate before build starts. Proposal on the table: Orkhan — build to launched state (the zero-to-launch role already defined for him); Alexandra — launch-to-traction owner — retention is her new remit and this is the retention product; her capacity frees as the onboarding-campaign work wraps (~Aug 10), and the explicit trade is: this pilot is her next retention project, not an addition to it; Cyril — commercial (positioning, pricing validation, first sales); Anton — spec and decisions. The honest caveat stays: launch-to-traction is the phase MessageHub died in and nobody on the team has carried it yet — which is why the meeting ratifies a named owner with a named capacity trade, or we don't build.

Isn't this MessageHub again?

MessageHub died of orphanhood, not of weak demand. The protection here is not optimism: owners assigned per stage at this meeting (see above — if we can't name them, we don't build), kill criteria fixed before launch (Section 4), and distribution into an audience we already have in-app rather than a cold launch.

Personal WhatsApp via QR, or the official WhatsApp Business API (WABA)?

Both, QR first — we do not start WABA-only. V1 launches on the QR/personal-number path: it's the adoption route every winning product in this market ships (see the matrix), it's TimelinesAI's core competence, and it means zero Meta setup, approvals, or per-message fees for the exact small-team segment we target. QR carries Meta-ban risk; we take that risk deliberately and mitigate it in-product (Section 11, capability M12). Coexistence is the bridge, not an alternative start: when the platform's WABA API ships (S1), a WhatsApp-Business-app number connects to the official Cloud API via Meta's Coexistence mode — the phone app keeps working while the number gains official-API status. That's the compliant upgrade path we offer existing users, without asking anyone to give up their phone.

Will it cannibalize the core product?

The target user doesn't have a CRM and isn't buying the core product today. The path runs toward the core: a Timelines CRM customer who outgrows it and adopts a real CRM is a warm buyer of the bridge we already sell. Honest limit: until L6 ships, graduation means signing up for the core product — pipeline data doesn't migrate yet, so we sell graduation as a story, not a button.

Why would anyone pick us over Kommo or Privyr?

Distribution and friction, not features. We already have the audience in-app (the no-CRM majority of our base), the WhatsApp sync tech is ours, and we sell monthly self-serve where the market leader locks buyers into 6–24-month upfront contracts (Section 8).

What if it doesn't work?

We stop — kill criteria in Section 4, sunk cost kept small by design.

3. Problem and evidence

  • 59% of newly WhatsApp-activated workspaces never connect any CRM (cohort that connected WhatsApp 1–4 months ago, N≈3,000, test accounts excluded — prod DB, Jul-2026). Among the surviving active base, only 34% are CRM-less: no-CRM workspaces don't stick around. The 59→34 gap is the leak this product buys back.
  • CRM-connected users convert to paid ~5–8× better than non-CRM users (internal funnel data, Jun-2026). Honest caveat: this is correlation — CRM-connectors are self-selected, more-committed buyers. Whether giving no-CRM users our CRM reproduces the lift is exactly what the pilot's matched-cohort retention test answers. The non-CRM cohort is our biggest and leakiest segment — distinct from the ban-driven churn reason discussed in Section 11.
  • These users don't want "a CRM". They want their WhatsApp chats to stop losing them money: know who's a lead, what stage they're at, and when to follow up.
  • The market proves they pay for exactly this: Kommo 500k+ users, Privyr 500k+ claimed businesses, SocialHub 15k companies (Brazil), Interakt 10k businesses (India). Full analysis: the market research page; pricing anchors in Section 8.

4. Goals, metrics, kill criteria (proposal — to be fixed at the decision meeting)

Primary goal: retain the no-CRM segment. Secondary: standalone MRR; warm pipeline into the core product.

Primary pilot metric — activation and retention lift, not MRR. The pilot audience is mostly our own paying base, which gets the product free (Section 8), so by design it cannot produce MRR; judging the pilot on MRR would rig it to fail.

  • Activation definition: workspace connected a WhatsApp number + created a pipeline + promoted ≥5 chats into Deals + returned in week 2.
  • 90-day pilot targets: ≥100 activated workspaces; measurable retention lift in the activated no-CRM cohort vs. the matched non-activated cohort.
  • Paying cohort (trialing and churned no-CRM workspaces — the ones who are charged during the pilot; active payers are free, net-new channels open after the scale decision): ≥20 paying workspaces and ≥$500 standalone MRR as a secondary, directional signal — not a co-equal gate. Seat math: $19 at 3 seats, +$5/seat → $29 at 5 seats; the gate assumes a ~$25 blended average, so 20 paying ≈ $500.
  • Guardrails: no measurable churn increase in the core product; support load stays within the existing team's capacity.

Kill criteria: if 90 days after launch there are <30 activated workspaces, or the paying cohort is <8 workspaces with a flat trajectory — stop investment, park the product, write down what we learned. The middle band (30–99 activated, or 8–19 paying with a rising trajectory): extend the pilot once by 45 days with a named fix hypothesis; a second inconclusive read counts as a park. To be agreed at the meeting so the later decision is mechanical, not emotional.

5. Target customer

A 1–5 person sales team (or a solo seller) in a WhatsApp-heavy market — LATAM, India, SEA, MENA — that runs all sales conversations on WhatsApp and has never adopted a CRM. The Privyr persona: "the salesperson who lives in WhatsApp and will never open a desktop CRM."

Reachable today, in order of cost: (1) current TimelinesAI workspaces (paying + trialing) with no CRM connected; (2) churned TimelinesAI users whose exit reason was "too complex / didn't need the bridge"; (3) net-new signups from listings and content — after the scale decision.

6. Market evidence (what actually drives v1 decisions)

Three findings from the market research (10 products deep-dived, 41 surveyed) shape this document:

  1. The winning feature set is small — inbox, auto-created contacts, kanban pipeline, reminders, templates. Nobody clones Pipedrive; Callbell wins without even deal amounts or a tasks module.
  2. QR is the adoption path. Products that are official-API-only carry setup friction, Meta per-message fees, and seat minimums; products that lead with QR (Kommo, Privyr, Agendor, TeleCRM) win the small-team segment. Privyr reached 500k businesses personal-number-first.
  3. AI field-extraction is the emerging differentiator (Moskit "Smart Fields"): AI reads the conversation and fills the CRM fields. Nobody does it well yet; it fits our stack.

7. Scope

Non-goals (v1):

  • Not a Pipedrive clone: no quotes/proposals, product catalogs, forecasting, email sequences, custom dashboards.
  • No changes to the core TimelinesAI product — with one carved exception: a single small placement (menu entry / banner shown to no-CRM workspaces), which is an explicit R&D ask (Section 10).
  • Not omnichannel: WhatsApp only (no Instagram/Messenger/Telegram) in v1.
  • Not sales-led: self-serve only; no demo-gated pricing, no mandatory onboarding calls.
  • No new brand: TimelinesAI-branded, on crm.timelines.ai.
  • No bulk or cold-outreach features of any kind in v1 — this is a ban-risk decision, not a roadmap accident (Section 11, M12).

In scope (v1): everything in Section 12 marked Must.

8. Business model and pricing (proposal)

  • Self-serve, monthly billing, free 14-day trial, card only at subscribe.
  • Standalone: $19/workspace/month including 3 seats, +$5 per extra seat. Within the market's workspace band ($18–97/mo; per-seat products charge $6–25) and under Kommo's effective entry: 3 seats × $15 sold only as 6–24-month upfront contracts. Monthly self-serve is the deliberate contrast.
  • Who is charged: active paying TimelinesAI workspaces — included at no extra charge (the retention play comes first). Trialing, churned, and net-new users — standalone price. Whether existing customers stay free after the pilot is an open decision (Section 11).
  • Willingness-to-pay is validated in the pilot, not asserted: three tests on the charged cohort — $19 vs $29 anchor split, local-currency display (MXN/BRL/INR) vs USD, and a "what would you pay" probe in onboarding. Owner: Cyril. $19 is the starting anchor, not the conclusion.
  • Billing rides the existing TimelinesAI workspace billing as an addon SKU — no separate billing system (see M9). A Timelines CRM user is a TimelinesAI workspace under the hood, so "recognizing" paying customers is automatic by construction.
  • Official-API usage (when added): Meta conversation fees passed through at cost, no markup in v1.

9. Distribution

Pilot channels, in order:

  1. In-app placement in TimelinesAI for workspaces with no CRM connected — the biggest and cheapest channel, but it is a core-product change: small, and it needs an R&D slot (Section 10). Fallback if R&D can't schedule it this quarter: lifecycle email becomes the primary channel.
  2. Lifecycle emails to the same segment (Customer.io).
  3. WabaLoop (our automated WhatsApp-ban outreach engine) and outbound sequences add it as an offer where relevant.
  4. Cyril offers it in sales conversations where the prospect has no CRM.

No paid acquisition, no marketplace listings during pilot — those come after the scale decision.

The growth plan to 100 activated workspaces (the arithmetic, from verified base numbers):

  • Reachable audience over 90 days: 780 currently-active no-CRM workspaces (34% of the 2,265 active base) + ~590/month newly WhatsApp-activated no-CRM workspaces (59% of ~1,000/mo inflow) ≈ ~2,500 workspaces see the offer.
  • Funnel assumptions (conservative in-app benchmarks): ~10% try it (~250 workspaces open and connect) → ~40% of those reach activation via the M10 checklist (~100). Net: 100 activated = 4% of reach — an in-app offer to an audience that already uses the platform daily, not a cold funnel.
  • Sequencing: weeks 1–2 — soft launch to ~50 hand-picked workspaces (Cyril + onboarding invitations), fix the sharpest onboarding friction; weeks 3–12 — in-app placement + two email waves per month to the segment.
  • Milestones: day 30 ≥ 25 activated · day 60 ≥ 60 · day 90 ≥ 100. Contingency if day-30 misses: open the churned-no-CRM email list (thousands of contacts), add Cyril demo pushes and a WabaLoop insert; if the in-app slot slips, email waves double in cadence as the primary channel.

Technical shape (what the seam confirmation is against): CRM state (deals, stages, fields, tags, notes, reminders) lives in the app's own database — same stack as the shipped integrations (TypeScript / Netlify / Neon Postgres); no CRM state in the core product. The app consumes the existing seam: inbound message webhooks, send-message API, contact/chat read, history fetch on connect. Volume envelope at pilot scale (100 workspaces): roughly 50 active chats × 30 messages/day each ≈ 150k message events/day ≈ 2–5 events/sec peak — modest against what the integrations already handle; AI extraction reads each conversation incrementally, not as bulk re-scans. This is the one-pager Igor confirms or corrects at the meeting.

AI cost and provider (M11 — summaries + field extraction): the workload runs on a small-model class (reference: Anthropic Claude Haiku 4.5 at $1 input / $5 output per million tokens; the equivalent mini-class models from other providers price similarly). Cost controls are structural: AI runs on Deals only (not every chat), extraction is incremental per new message (no bulk re-scans), and conversation prefixes are prompt-cached (~0.1× on re-read input). Generous per-workspace envelope — ~900 summary refreshes + ~3,000 extraction events/month ≈ 3.6M input + 0.3M output tokens — prices at ≈$5/workspace/month uncached worst case, ≈$0.5–1.5 realistic with caching and deal-only scope — i.e. 3–8% of the $19 price as AI COGS; the pilot's whole AI bill at 100 activated workspaces is ≈$100–150/month. A per-workspace daily AI budget cap ships in v1 as the abuse guard. Provider is chosen by the extraction-quality eval in the Calypso prototype, not by price — the candidates cost within cents of each other; accuracy on real WhatsApp conversations (multilingual, informal) is the deciding criterion.

11. Risks and open decisions

Risks:

  • Meta bans on QR sessions. Bans are the biggest churn reason in the core product today (exact share was debated internally — large either way). This product still leads with QR because it is the adoption path the whole market validates. Mitigations: v1 ships zero bulk/cold-outreach features (nothing to get banned for — M12), onboarding teaches safe usage and Meta's coexistence option, and the official-API path opens as soon as the WABA API ships. Pilot scale is capped by design, which caps exposure.
  • Billing. The addon SKU touches core billing, which currently has a live defect (~400–500 workspaces shown false "unpaid" banners) — coordinate with that fix rather than piling onto it. Never a launch blocker: the pilot can run free (Section 10).
  • Ownership gap. The failure mode that killed MessageHub. Closed only by the meeting naming owners with freed capacity — build does not start before that.
  • Support load lands on the existing support team; M10 (self-serve onboarding) is the guardrail.

Open decisions (the meeting must close 1–3; 4–5 can wait):

  1. Ratify the owner proposal (FAQ above): Orkhan builds to launch; Alexandra owns launch-to-traction with the named capacity trade; Cyril commercial. If the room can't ratify an owner for launch-to-traction, we don't build — that's the MessageHub lesson applied.
  2. Accept the bounded R&D scope: confirm the technical shape above (the seam), schedule the workspace-on-install fix on the critical path to "launched", grant the small in-app placement slot. Tracked but not forced: WABA date, billing SKU sequencing.
  3. Approve the pilot design: gates and kill criteria (Section 4), the pricing tests (Section 8), and the naming direction — "Timelines CRM" contains the word the positioning avoids (the market's own lesson); settle the customer-facing name before Cyril positions anything.
  4. Bundled-free vs paid for existing TimelinesAI customers after the pilot.
  5. Later channels (Instagram/Messenger) — only after the scale decision.
